Our study of discrete math begins with logic, which is how we reason about things that are either true or false.

Propositions and predicates

A true/false variable is called a boolean variable (named after George Boole). A statement that's either true or false is called a proposition. For example, "1 + 1 = 2" is a proposition that is true. A predicate is a proposition that depends on some variable or variables. For example, "$x$ was born in December" is a predicate whose value depends on who $x$ is.

Boolean functions

A boolean function is a function that takes in one or more boolean variables, and outputs either true or false. The most common functions are not, and, or, xor, and implies.

Not

Not is very simple. Suppose we have a proposition, "I was born in December." Let's call this proposition $p$. Then not $p$ is something like "I was not born in December." Something that is not true is false, and something that is not false is true.

The common mathematical notation for not is $\neg p$.

Or

When discussing logic, or means something a little bit different than how we normally use it. For example, when you say, "You can have a slice of cake for dessert, or you can have a slice of pie," you don't mean they can have both. This is what xor (exclusive-or) is. $p$ xor $q$ (notated $p \oplus q$) is only true if $p$ is true, or $q$ is true, but not both. Combining functions

We can combine boolean functions together to form new functions. For example, we can combine not and and by saying $\neg (p \land q)$. Two functions are equal if they have the same output for every possible input -- in other words, if they have the same truth table.

It's important to use parentheses to specify the order of operations, if it wouldn't be clear otherwise. For example, if we just wrote $\neg p \land q$, it's not clear if we mean $\neg (p \land q)$ or $(\neg p) \land q$. It makes a difference! We will show this using our first type of proof: proof by truth table.

Identities

Just as we have identities in algebra, we have certain identities in boolean algebra. For example, $\neg (p \land q)$ is, in fact, equal to $(\neg p) \lor (\neg q)$. This is one of DeMorgan's laws, the other one being $\neg (p \lor q) = (\neg p) \land (\neg q)$. We'll prove the first one by truth table.

Using a truth table is a good way to prove DeMorgan's laws, but it doesn't offer much insight into why it's true. It can be fun to think through these things using real world scenarios.

Let's say we're throwing a party, and we invited our friends, Penelope and Quentin, who recently broke up with each other. Let $p$ be "Penelope attends the party" and $q$ be "Quentin attends the party." It would be be awkward if they both showed up. So, the party won't be awkward as long as $\neg (p \land q)$. Another way to think about it is that it's okay as long as Penelope doesn't show up, or Quentin doesn't show up, or neither shows up. In other words, $(\neg p) \lor (\neg q)$.

Common identities

A list of common identities is below. These can all be proven using truth tables.

Associativity
$(p \land q) \land r = p \land (q \land r) \\ (p \lor q) \lor r = p \lor (q \lor r)$
Distributivity
$p \land (q \lor r) = (p \land q) \lor (p \land q) \\ p \lor (q \land r) = (p \lor q) \land (p \lor q)$
Idempotency
$p \land p = p \\ p \lor p = p$
Double negation
$\neg (\neg p) = p$
DeMorgan's laws
$\neg (p \land q) = (\neg p) \lor (\neg q) \\ \neg (p \lor q) = (\neg p) \land (\neg q)$
Commutativity
$p \land q = q \land p \\ p \lor q = q \lor p$
Absorption
$p \lor (p \land q) = p \\ p \land (p \lor q) = p$

Manipulating equations

We can use these identities to manipulate boolean equations algebraically. As an example, let's prove that $(p \land q) \lor p = p \land (q \lor p)$.

Proof:

StepReason
$(p \land q) \lor p = p \lor (p \land q)$Commutativity
$p \lor (p \land q) = (p \lor p) \land (p \lor q)$Distributivity
$(p \lor p) \land (p \lor q) = p \land (p \lor q)$Idempotency
$p \land (p \lor q) = p \land (q \lor p)$Commutativity $\square$

You can also prove that the two functions are true by showing that $(p \land q) \lor p = p$ and $p \land (q \lor p) = p$ through commutativity and absorption.

Quantifiers

Remember that predicates are true or false statements that take on some variable or variables, like $p(x)$ = "$x$ was born in December." There are two quantifiers we can add to this: for all, and there exists.

For all is notated with this symbol: $\forall$. For example, if $p(x)$ = "$x$ was born in December," then $\forall x, p(x)$ means "for all $x$, $x$ was born in December." Note that not everyone is born in December, so that statement is most likely false.

There exists is notated with this symbol: $\exists$. We usually write statements like "$\exists x$ such that $p(x)$." Often "such that" is abbreviated as s.t., or is just replaced entirely with a dot, like $\exists x . p(x)$.

Negating quantifiers

The opposite of $\forall x, p(x)$ is $\exists x . \neg p(x)$. For example, say $p(x)$ = "$x$ can swim." Then the opposite of "for all $x$, $x$ can swim" is "there exists some $x$ such that $x$ can't swim."

Similarly, the opposite of $\exists x . p(x)$ is $\forall x, \neg p(x)$. For example, let $p(x)$ = "$x$ has three eyes." Then the opposite of "there exists an $x$ such that $x$ has three eyes" is "for all $x$, $x$ does not have three eyes."
