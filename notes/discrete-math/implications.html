Implies is one of the most frequently used boolean functions. Intuitively, "$p$ implies $q$", means that $q$ logically follows from $p$. This is important, because it gives us the building block of proofs. For example:

  1. $x$ is a human implies $x$ is mortal
  2. Socrates is a human
  3. Therefore, Socrates is mortal

There are many different ways to say $p$ implies $q$ (notated $p \to q$):

  • $p$ implies $q$
  • $p \to q$
  • If $p$, then $q$
  • $q$ if $p$
  • $p$ is sufficient for $q$
  • $q$ is necessary for $p$

Check to see if those make sense to you. "If $p$, then $q$" is probably the most common alternative way to state $p$ implies $q$.

Importantly, if $p$ is false, then the implies function is true by default, regardless of what $q$ is. This is kind of like saying, "If pigs fly, then I'll give you a million dollars." Saying this wouldn't be a lie since pigs will never fly.

Examples

Some examples may help clarify how this works.

Number line

Suppose $p$ = "$x > 5$" and $q$ = "$x > 0$". In this case, $p$ implies $q$ for all values of $x$, since a number greater than 5 must also be greater than 0.

Now suppose $p$ = "$x > -5$" and $q$ = "$x > 0$". Does $p$ imply $q$ for all values of $x$? No. For example, if $x = -3$, then $p$ is true, but $q$ is false, so $p$ does not imply $q$.

Checking IDs

Imagine being in a restaurant, and let $p$ = "The customer is drinking something alcoholic" and $q$ = "The customer is aged 21 or over." You see four customers with drinks:

  1. One is drinking a beer.
  2. One is drinking a soda.
  3. One is age 25.
  4. One is age 18.

Whose drinks/IDs do you need to check to see if $p \to q$ for every customer? Let's check the truth table:

$p$$q$$p \to q$
Drinking beer25 year oldtrue
Drinking beer18 year oldfalse
Drinking soda25 year oldtrue
Drinking soda18 year oldtrue

As you can see, $p \to q$ is only false if the customer is drinking a beer and is 18, so you should check the customer drinking a beer, and the customer who's 18. Notice that if $p$ is false (the customer's drinking a soda), then $p \to q$ = true no matter what $q$ is.

Inverse, converse, and contrapositive

There are special names for when you rearrange or negate $p$ and $q$. Given $p \to q$:

  • $\neg p \to \neg q$ is the inverse.
  • $q \to p$ is the converse.
  • $\neg q \to \neg p$ is the contrapositive.

Example

Suppose your friend tells you, "if it's sunny tomorrow, then I'll go for a run." Let's also say that there are only two possibilities for the weather: sun or rain. Here are the inverse, converse, and contrapositive of that statement:

  • Inverse: "If it rains tomorrow, then I won't go for a run."
  • Converse: "If I go for a run tomorrow, then it's sunny."
  • Contrapositive: "If I don't go for a run tomorrow, then it rained."

Questions:

  1. What is the inverse of "If a customer is drinking alcohol, he must be 21 or over"?

  2. What is the converse of "If a customer is drinking alcohol, he must be 21 or over"?

  3. What is the contrapositive of "If a customer is drinking alcohol, he must be 21 or over"?

If your friend keeps her word, which of the three statements above have to be true? Surprisingly, only the contrapositive has to be true.

It could be the case that your friend just loves to run, rain or shine. She told you that if it didn't rain tomorrow, she would go for a run. But if it does rain, she could go for a run anyway. Assuming the inverse is always true is called the inverse error.

The converse isn't guaranteed to be true for the same reason. Just because your friend went for a run doesn't mean it was sunny, since she could have run in the rain. Assuming the converse is always true is called the converse error.

Why is the contrapositive always true? If it was sunny outside, then your friend would have gone for her run. But if it didn't rain, then we don't know what her plans are. She may have gone for arun in the rain, but she also might have just stayed at home. Still, the only time your friend wouldn't have gone for a run is if it was rainy outside.
