When two boolean expressions have the same truth table, they're equivalent. In this section, we'll prove certain facts about boolean logic that will be helpful.

If and only if

If $A \to B$ and $B \to A$, we say $A$ "if and only if" $B$ (denoted $A \leftrightarrow B$).

{% include truth-table.html id="iff" vars="['A', 'B']" exprs="[new TTImplies('A', 'B'), new TTImplies('B', 'A'), new TTAnd(new TTImplies('A', 'B'), new TTImplies('B', 'A')), new TTIff('A', 'B')]" hiddenExprs="{}" %}

As we can see from the truth table, what this really means is that if $A \leftrightarrow B$ is true, then $A$ and $B$ are the same, and vice versa.

De Morgan's laws

One practical identity in boolean logic tells us how to negate an expression. The laws are:

$$ \begin{align} \neg (A \land B) &= (\neg A \lor \neg B) \\ \neg (A \lor B) &= (\neg A \land \neg B) \end{align} $$

To show that the first equation is correct, we'll fill out the truth table for $\neg (A \land B)$, and the truth table for $(\neg A \lor \neg B)$. The two expressions have the same truth values for all values of $A$ and $B$.

Inverse, converse, and contrapositive revisited

In the notes on implications, we saw that the inverse and converse of an implication are not necessarily true, but the contrapositive is. As a reminder:

  • Inverse: $\neg A \to \neg B$
  • Converse: $B \to A$
  • Contrapositive: $\neg B \to \neg A$

Notice that the truth tables for the inverse and converse are not the same as for an implication. However, the inverse and converse are equivalent to each other:

    Does this seem familiar? Something similar is true in basic algebra: $A \cdot (B + C) = A \cdot B + A \cdot C$. This is known as the distributive property (see link for a full list of rules). It turns out, boolean logic and arithmetic share the same underlying mathematical structure. You can learn more by reading the Wikipedia article on boolean algebra, or taking a class in abstract algebra.
